Abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable success, yet their performance is often highly sensitive to the structure of input prompts. This dependency creates a challenge for real-world applications where inputs are inherently complex and noisy. Although it is commonly believed that more detailed prompts yield better results, we experimentally uncover a critical paradox: providing excessively complex or poorly structured information can severely degrade, rather than enhance, model performance. We term this vulnerability cognitive overload. The existence of this phenomenon is crucial, as it limits the reliability of LLMs in high-stakes, information-rich scenarios. To address this, we first introduce CogniLoad, a systematic benchmark that operationalizes four types of cognitive load to diagnose this issue. Our analysis with CogniLoad reveals two distinct failure modes, which we link to the model's Contextual Memory and Parametric Memory. Building on this diagnosis, we then propose the Cognitive Orchestration Framework (COF), a novel, training-free multi-agent architecture. COF mitigates cognitive overload at runtime by emulating collaborative problem-solving through task decomposition and a shared collective memory. Extensive experiments across three major datasets demonstrate that our framework significantly restores reasoning performance on high-load tasks where strong baseline methods like Chain-of-Thought fail.
